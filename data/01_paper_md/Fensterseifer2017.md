## **2. Data and methods**

#### 2.1. Field experiment data

The data comprised four growing seasons of field experiment (2010/2011- 2013/2014) conducted at eight different locations in the Rio Grande do Sul (RS) State [(Fig.](#page-2-0) 1). The experiments started at Santa Maria in 2010/2011 and gradually replicated to other regions. Each experimental site represent different soybean growing region of the State. Tupancireta and Julio de Castihos rank as the first and the third larger soybean grown area in the state. Agua Santa represents the most soybean productive region. In Itaqui and Pelotas, farmers are growing soybean only to fit in their crop rotations with irrigated rice in lowlands, to reduce weed infestation and improve soil fertility. Frederico Westphalen was selected to participate because it is a city in the Northwest region which also has a federal university. SantaMaria andRestinga Seca aremostly counties with small farming systems. The soybean variety BMX Potência (Maturity group VI) was chosen for this study, because was one of the most planted soybean genotype in the south of Brazil in past three years (2012–2014).

The experimental design was a complete randomized block, with four replications. Each replication was a plot with 15 rows, each row with 3 m long and 0.45 m between rows (plant density of 31/m2). The soybean developmental stages R1 (beginning bloom), R3 (beginning pod), R5 (beginning seed) and R7 (beginning maturity) were measured according to the [Fehr](#page-6-0) [and](#page-6-0) [Caviness](#page-6-0) [(1977)](#page-6-0) scale. The final leaf number was obtained when the leaf appearance stopped and the Leaf area index (LAI) was measured twice a month. The canopy height was measured when the plants were ready to be harvested (R8). The number of grains per meter square was counted atthe harvest and also a sample with a hundred grains was separated and oven dried at 60 ◦C to determine the dry mass of one grain. Finally, the grain yield was obtained on a 13% moisture basis. The detailed information on seasons, locations, sowing dates, irrigation records, measurements interval and observation intervals are in [Table](#page-2-0) 1, [Richter](#page-6-0) et [al.,](#page-6-0) [2014](#page-6-0) and [Zanon](#page-6-0) et [al.,](#page-6-0) [2015](#page-6-0) a, b.

#### 2.2. Climate and soil data

The Rio Grande do Sul lies in southern part of Brazil. The climate is humid subtropical with 86.7 percent with hot summer (Cfa) and 13.3 percent with temperate summer (Cfb) based on Köppen system climate classification ([Alvares](#page-6-0) et [al.,](#page-6-0) [2013).](#page-6-0)

# 2.2.1. Climate data

The daily weather data for maximum and minimum temperature (TMax and TMin) solar radiation (SRad) and precipitation (Rain) used in this study were collected from National Institute of Meteorology (INPE), Brazilian Agriculture Research Corporation (Embrapa) or Foundation of livestock and agriculture research of the State (Fepagro). Since all the experimental sites do not have weather stations, especially Agua Santa, Itaqui and tupancireta, most nearest weather stations, Passo Fundo, Uruguaina and Julio de Castihos station weather data has been used, respectively. The details on seasonal weather average prevailed were listed in [Table](#page-3-0) 2.

### 2.2.2. Soil data

The physical characteristics of upper layer top soil (0–20 cm) were analyzed through soil sampling from all sites. Information from deeper layers were obtained traced from RADAMBrasil(1986) project. Following the Global Reanalysis Soil Database Methods ([Romero](#page-6-0) et [al.,](#page-6-0) [2012),](#page-6-0) [the](#page-6-0) [Saxton](#page-6-0) [method](#page-6-0) [(1986)](#page-6-0) was applied to estimate soil bulk density, saturated hydraulic conductivity and saturation point. Further, for the specific soils of Rio Grande do Sul, [Reichert](#page-6-0) et [al.](#page-6-0) [(2009)](#page-6-0) pedotrasfer functions were used to compute permanent wilting point (PWP) and field capacity (FC). The details on soil were presented on [Table](#page-3-0) 3.

## 2.3. Grouping experiments approach

All together, there were twenty-one different experiments resulted from locations, years and sowing dates combinations used in this study. These experiments were organized into four different groups:

#### 2.3.1. Group 1: individual sowings

In this group, the twenty-one experiments were used to calibrate the model separately, one by one. Each time, one experiment was used to calibrate and the remaining 20 experiments were used as validation. This approach resulted in 21 sets of calibrated genetic coefficients.

# 2.3.2. Group 2: season/year per location

The sowing dates within a growing season conducted at a particular location were aggregated to calibrate the crop model. For instance, in Santa Maria, there were four sowing dates in the year 2012/13, thus all the four experiments were used to calibrate the phenological and growth genetic coefficients, and the remaining 17 experiments were used as validation. There were five different field experiment datasets with 2 (experiments 1 and 2), 3 (experiments 3, 4 and 5), 4 (experiments 6, 7, 8 and 9), 3 (experiments 10, 11 and 12) and 3 (experiments 17, 18 and 19) experiments [(Table](#page-2-0) 1).

## 2.3.3. Group 3: experimental sites

All the available experiments within a given location were clustered, including planting dates and growing seasons. There were two datasets in this grouping level: one with 12 experiments (1–12 conducted at Santa Maria) and other with 3 experiments (17–19 conducted at Itaqui) [(Table](#page-2-0) 1). In this case, the first dataset (12 experiments) were used to calibrate the phenological and growth genetic coefficients, and the remaining 9 experiments were used as validation. In the second dataset, 3 experiments were used to calibrate and the remaining 18 experiments, as validation.

# 2.3.4. Group 4: all data together

Temporal integration using different planting dates and seasons, and spatial integration using different locations. All available twenty-one experiments were used, to calibrate and validate the model.

The cross-validation leave-one-out (LOO) method [(Efron](#page-6-0) [and](#page-6-0) [Gong,](#page-6-0) [1983;](#page-6-0) [Efron](#page-6-0) [and](#page-6-0) [Tibshirani,](#page-6-0) [1998;](#page-6-0) [Baigorria](#page-6-0) et [al.,](#page-6-0) [2010)](#page-6-0) is a statistical procedure that allows to use as many sets of observed experimental data as possible. This method allowed to calibrate/validate 21 experimental datasets (N) in 21 different repetitions (k) (N = k). In each k repetition, 20 experimental datasets were used to calibrate and the remaining one to validate as an independent dataset. In the end, each repetition k was conducted without an exclusive experimental dataset until all the 21 experiments were left out once. The calibration/validation procedure always started from EM-PL coefficient and kept moving forward until the last one was calibrated (FL-SH, FS-SD, SD-PM, FL-LF, LFMAX, SLAVR, SIZLF, XFRT, WTPSD, PODUR and SFDUR). Some genetic coefficients can affect not one but several growth and/or development variables; Therefore, each time a genetic coefficient was calibrated,the Relative Root Mean Square Errors obtained in all variables (Emergence, R1, R3, R5, R7, Final Leaf Number, Leaf Area Index, Canopy Height, One Grain dry mass, Grains.m−2 and Yield) were re-analyzed. The cross-validation was applied in all groups described in section [2.3.](#page-1-0)

$$\text{RRMSE} = \left(\frac{\sqrt{\sum \frac{\left(\text{oin} - \text{sif}\right)^2}{n}}}{\theta}\right) * 100.$$

Where s is the simulated and o the observed value in each experiment i, n is number of measurements in each variable and is the average of all observed values. When the calibration/validation procedure was completed, it produced 21 best values for each one of the 12 genetic coefficients, and then, among the 21 values, the most frequent value was considered as the final value of genetic coefficient.
