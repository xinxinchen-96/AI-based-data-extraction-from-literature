| Crop simulation models offer possibilities to evaluate and target agricultural information for sustainable in<br>tensification in countries like Ethiopia with inadequate resources for field research. The objectives of this re<br>search were to calibrate and evaluate the CERES-Maize, CROPGRO-Dry bean and CROPGRO-Soybean models for<br>practices associated with conservation agriculture and fertilizer N, and to evaluate five generated weather da<br>tasets for Ethiopia. Data from multi-year field experiments and additional data obtained from previously con<br>ducted national variety trials were used for model evaluation. Generated weather datasets for six agroecologies<br>were evaluated by comparison with observed data and by use of data in the models. Genetic coefficients used in<br>the models for maize, dry bean and soybean were determined by model parametrization and calibration of<br>phenology and yield. The models acceptably simulated the effects of N rate, maize-legume rotation, and crop<br>residue retention plus tillage with average normalized deviation closer to zero, RMSE less or similar to standard<br>deviation of observed data, and with normalized RMSE (nRMSE) < 15%. Both NASA and Global Yield Gap Atlas<br>(GYGA) daily rainfall showed good agreement with observed weather data (RMSE < 9 mm). Daily maximum<br>and minimum temperature of GYGA and WeatherMan datasets had the lowest RMSE of 1.99 and 3.06, and 2.5<br>and 3.1 °C, respectively. Between 85–100% of simulated grain yields of maize, dry bean and soybean with GYGA<br>and WeatherMan datasets fell within ± 10% deviation of mean simulated grain yields with observed weather<br>data, and with the lowest inter-annual variability. It was concluded that model calibrations were satisfactory,<br>and either GYGA or WeatherMan datasets alone or combined could be used to run the crop models in sites which<br>lack observed daily weather data in Ethiopia. |

## 2. Materials and methods

# 2.1. Field experiments

# 2.1.1. Experiment-I

Field experiments were conducted in 2010–2016 and 2015–2016, respectively, at Melkassa and Bako Agricultural Research Centers in Ethiopia, hereafter referred to as Melkassa and Bako, to calibrate CERES-Maize, CROPGRO-Dry bean and CROPGRO-Soybean. These experiments provided data comparing: no-tillage with 100% crop residue retention and plow tillage with complete residue removal; maize (Zea mays L.) monoculture, dry bean (Phaseolus vulgaris L.) monoculture, maize-dry bean rotation, and maize-dry bean intercropping at Melkassa; and the two-tillage practices with maize monoculture, soybean (Glycine max L.) monoculture, maize-soybean rotation, soybeanmaize rotation, and maize-soybean intercropping at Bako in a complete factorial split-plot design with tillage assigned to the main plots and cropping systems to the sub-plots. The plot area was 100 m2 for both maize and legumes.

Tillage was according to the local practice of tilling three times using the oxen drawn "maresha" plow ([Melesse, 2007](#page-11-20)). The first and second tillage were, respectively, done in the first and second week of May at Bako and in the first and second week of June at Melkassa while the third tillage was done at planting time. Planting was from the last week of May to mid-June at Bako for both crops, and in the third and fourth wk of June for maize and in the last week of June and first week of July for dry bean at Melkassa.

The maize cultivars were the hybrid BH546 at Bako and the open pollinated Melkassa-II at Melkassa. Soybean cv Dhidhesa and dry bean cv Nassir were grown at Bako and Melkassa, respectively. The inter-and intra-row spacing were 0.75 x 0.30 m, 0.75 x 0.25 m, 0.60 x 0.10 m and 0.40 x 0.10 m, respectively, for BH546, Melkassa-II, Dhidhesa, and Nassir. The fertilizers applied were diammonium phosphate (DAP, 18- 46-0) and urea (46-0-0). For treatments with maize, 46 kg P2O5 ha−1 and 41 kg N ha-1 were applied at planting and 23 kg N ha−1 at maize 5 leaf stage at Bako, and 46 kg P2O5 ha−1 and 30 kg N ha−1 were applied at maize planting plus 12 kg N ha−1 applied at the 5-leaf stage at Melkassa. Only 46 kg P2O5 ha−1 was applied at planting to soybean and dry bean monoculture, and when the rotation crop was a legume. Glyphosate was applied at 3 l ha-1 at two wk before planting for the notillage plots to control weeds, followed by hand weeding as needed throughout crop growth. Weeds were controlled using hand hoes in tilled plots.

Crop management information needed to run CERES-Maize, CROPGRO-Dry bean and CROPGRO-Soybean were recorded during the experiment at both study sites. Residue samples were collected from all main plots at 2 wk before planting and analyzed. Crop residue N was 0.44% for Bako and 0.39% for Melkassa maize residue, 0.35% for soybean residue, and 0.33% for dry bean residue. Two wk before planting, roots of maize or legume from 4 m2 area in the maize monoculture and maize-legume (dry bean or soybean) rotation plots were dug out, thoroughly washed, and oven dried at 60 °C to constant weight. Means of estimated root residue were 92, 78, 37 and 33 kg ha−1 for BH546, Melkassa-II, Dhidhesa and Nassir cultivars, respectively. In addition, crop residue maintained on the monoculture and rotation systems plots where sampled for a 4 m2 area by tracking and removing roots to a 0.5 m depth and oven dried at 60 °C to constant weight at 2 wk before maize or legume planting. Means of residue amounts were 2873 kg ha−1 for maize and 976 kg ha−1 for soybean at Bako, and 1723 kg ha−1 for maize and 1083 kg ha−1 for dry bean at Melkassa.

Time series data of leaf area index (LAI) and above ground biomass important for models calibration were collected in 2014 and 2016 at Melkassa and in 2015 and 2016 at Bako for both maize and legume cultivars. The time series data of LAI and above ground biomass were measured at the 5-leaf, 9-leaf, flowering (maximum LAI) and physiological maturity growth stages for BH546 and Melkassa-II. Maize kernel weight, kernel ear−1 , and grain yield were also obtained. Above ground biomass and LAI at 5-unrolled trifoliate, flowering and physiological maturity, and kernel weight, kernel pod−1 , and grain yield were obtained for dry bean and soybean. Leaf area was determined for five plants taken from middle two rows next to the border rows at the specified crop stages using LI-3100C Area Meter. The LAI was calculated as leaf area m-2 of ground area. Biomass of five sample plants at the specified crop stages were collected and oven dried at 60 °C to constant weight. Grain yield was determined for 120 g kg−1 water content. In addition, growth stage data such as days to emergence, flowering and physiological maturity for the crop years 2010–2016 at Melkassa and for 2015 and 2016 at Bako were also measured and used for the model calibration.

Soil profiles were characterized to 2.0 m depth at both locations and the hydraulic limits were generated based on the measured soil properties using the DSSAT internal pedotransfer functions ([Table 1](#page-3-0)). Three composite soil samples were analyzed for each soil profile. The laboratory analyses for ground and sieved samples included: particle size distribution by the hydrometer Bouyoucos method ([Van Reeuwijk,](#page-12-8) [1992](#page-12-8)); pH in 1:2.5 soil/water ratio after the soil suspension had been equilibrated at 25 ± °C ([Houba et al., 1989](#page-11-21)); determination of organic C by Walkley–Black wet digestion method ([Houba et al., 1989](#page-11-21)); total Kjeldahl N ([Houba et al., 1989)](#page-11-21); available P ([Olsen et al., 1954](#page-12-9)); and exchangeable cations by ammonium acetate extraction [(Van Reeuwijk,](#page-12-8) [1992](#page-12-8)). Daily weather data for rainfall, solar radiation (calculated from sunshine hours), and Tmax and Tmin air temperature were obtained from weather stations at the study sites. Simulated outputs obtained by running the model in seasonal and sequence analysis mode were used to evaluate the model performance under monoculture and rotation systems, respectively.

# 2.1.2. Experiment-II

Data from rainfed field experiments previously conducted by [Mes](#page-11-22)fin [(2017)](#page-11-22) to calibrate the APSIM crop simulation model for two N rates at Melkassa in 2013 and 2014 main crop seasons were used to evaluate CERES-Maize for low and high N rates. Maize cv Melkassa-II was grown under conventional practices and plant population as described for Experiment-I. Crop management information needed to run the crop model were taken from Mesfi[n (2017)](#page-11-22). Data from 0 and 100 kg N ha−1 for grain yield, above ground biomass, days to anthesis, and kernel wt. from the study were used for CERES-Maize evaluation. Model was run in seasonal mode to evaluate its performance with data from this experiment.

# 2.1.3. Experiment-III

A field experiment previously conducted at Melkassa by [Tadesse](#page-12-10) [and Kim (2015)](#page-12-10) was used to evaluate CERES-Maize model for six N rates. The N fertilizer levels tested in the study were 0, 25, 50, 75, 100 and 125 kg N ha−1 . The N fertilizer source was urea split applied at planting and at the 5-leaf stage. Maize cv hybrid MHQ138 adapted at 1550 m elevation was grown. The net plot area was 11.47 m2 , inter-row spacing was 0.75 m, and population density was 5.3 plants m-2. Planting occurred on 26 June 2014 and harvest on 21 November 2014. All other crop management information needed to run the crop model is available in [Tadesse and Kim (2015)](#page-12-10). The model was evaluated using measured data for biomass, grain N, grain yield, harvest index, kernel wt., and LAI for the six N rates. Model was run in seasonal mode to evaluate its performance with data from this experiment.

# 2.1.4. National variety trials

Past rainfed national variety trials with cv BH546, Melkassa-II, MHQ138, Dhidhesa and Nassir and with sufficient records of crop management information for crop growth model evaluation were conducted at Bako and Melkassa. Grain yield data of 10 yr for each Melkassa-II and Nassir and 6 yr of data for MHQ138 were collected from the selected national variety trials conducted at Melkassa. Also, 9 yr of data for BH546 and 8 yr of data for Dhidhesa were obtained from the national variety trials conducted at Bako. The grain yield data were used to evaluate performance of the calibrated CERES-Maize, CROPGRO-Dry bean and CROPGRO-Soybean. In addition, maize national variety trials conducted at Melkassa in 2012 and 2013 provided days to flowering and physiological maturity, kernel wt., kernel ear−1 , and grain yield data for determination of genetic coefficients of cv MHQ138. Tillage was conventional tillage as described in experiment-I for all national variety trials. Planting date and fertilizer rates were as described in Experiment-III for MHQ138 and as in Experiment-I for the other maize and legume varieties. Weeds were controlled by manual hand hoeing manually. Model was run in seasonal mode to evaluate its performance with data from this experiment.

#### 2.3. Statistical indices

Different indices were used to evaluate the performances of CERES-Maize, CROPGRO-Dry bean and CROPGRO-Soybean models imbedded in the DSSAT crop model package. A parametric procedure paired t-test [P(t)] was applied for testing whether the means of two samples drawn in pairs are different. An optimal model reproduces experimental data with P(t) > 0.05 ([Jing et al., 2016](#page-11-25)). P(t) larger than 0.05 shows observed and simulated data were not significantly different at the 95% confidence interval. The root mean square error (RMSE) measured the average magnitude of the estimate errors with lower RMSE values indicating greater central tendencies and generally smaller extreme errors ([Wallach, 2006](#page-12-12); [Yang et al., 2014](#page-12-13)). The RMSE between simulated and observed data is ideally less than or similar to the observed standard deviation (uncertainty of measured data) ([Gaydon et al., 2017](#page-11-2)). The normalized RMSE (nRMSE) gave a measure (%) of the relative difference of simulated versus observed data, and the simulation is considered excellent with nRMSE < 10%, good if between 10–20%, fair if between 20–30%, and poor if equal or greater than 30% ([Wallach and](#page-12-14) Goffi[net, 1987](#page-12-14); [Loague and Green, 1991](#page-11-26); [Wallach, 2006](#page-12-12)). Hence, RMSE and nRMSE between measured (Yi) and simulated (Zi) values were calculated using Eqs. (1) and (2), where n was the number of observations, and Ŷ and Ẑ were the average of measured and simulated values, respectively.

<span id="page-3-1"></span>
$$RMSE = \left(\frac{\sum \text{(Yi} - Zi)}{n}\right)^{0.5} \tag{1}$$

$$\text{nRMSE} = \frac{100 \times \text{RMSE}}{\text{Y}} \tag{2}$$

The model simulation efficiency or Nash–Sutcliff ;e Efficiency coefficient (EF) showed how well the generated data predicted the observed time series and varied from minus infinity to one. Negative EF meant that the observed mean was better than the generated estimate, EF = 0 indicated that the observed mean was as good as the estimated, and EF = 1 corresponded to a perfect match between observed measurements and model based estimates ([Wallach, 2006;](#page-12-12) [Tote et al.,](#page-12-15) <span id="page-3-2"></span>[2015)](#page-12-15).

$$\text{HF} = 1 - \frac{\Sigma(\text{Yi} - \text{Zi})^2}{\Sigma(\text{Yi} - \Sigma)^2} \tag{3}$$

The index of agreement (d) determined the degree of agreement between the predicted and their respective observed values and d = ∼1 is desirable ([Willmott, 1982;](#page-12-16) [Wallach, 2006)](#page-12-12). The mean error (ME) estimated the average generated error and a positive and negative ME shows that the generated data were generally overestimated and underestimated, respectively. The Bias reflected the degree to which the measured value was over- or under-estimated ([Wallach, 2006](#page-12-12)). Bias alone, however, was not sufficient as a summary of model errors as a bias value near zero could be due to very small model errors in all situations, or alternatively of large errors that approximately cancel out each other between under- and over-prediction. The Pearson correlation coefficient (r) was used to evaluate how well the generated corresponded to the observed ([Wallach, 2006](#page-12-12)).

For some case, normalized deviations (Zi-Yi)/Yi were also calculated to determine overall performance of the crop models ([Mitchell,](#page-11-27) [1997](#page-11-27)). A plot of normalized deviation against observed variable was used to detect patterns in predictive ability of the model ([Mitchell,](#page-11-27) [1997](#page-11-27); [Bonifas and Lindquist, 2006](#page-11-28)). The average of the normalized deviations provides an estimate similar to a coefficient of variation ([Lindquist, 2001b](#page-11-29)). Therefore, the smaller the average of the normalized deviations, the greater the accuracy in predicting observations. The sign of the average of the normalized deviations value ( ± ) indicates whether the model over- or under-predicts observations ([Bonifas and](#page-11-28) [Lindquist, 2006](#page-11-28)). For flood forecasting and hydrological purposes, it is important to avoid under-estimates of rainfall event amounts (ME < 0) but over-estimates must be avoided for drought monitoring (ME > 0) ([Dembele and Zwart, 2016](#page-11-30)). Climatology products with high r and EF and low RMSE have to be considered for general purposes ([Dembele](#page-11-30) [and Zwart, 2016](#page-11-30)).

$$d = 1 - \frac{(Y\bar{\imath} - Z\bar{\imath})^2}{\mathbb{E}\left(|Z\bar{\imath} - \hat{Y}| + |Y\bar{\imath} - \hat{Y}|\right)^2} \tag{4}$$

<span id="page-3-3"></span>
$$\text{ME} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{l=1}^{n} \text{ (Yi-Zi)}\tag{5}$$

#### 2.4. Weather datasets and their evaluation

# 3. Results

# 3.1. Genetic coefficients and model evaluation

Results of calibrated genetic coefficients for the CERES-Maize, CROPGRO-Dry bean and CROPGRO-Soybean are presented in [Table 3](#page-4-1). Models evaluation based on datasets from the national variety trials illustrated more than 95% of predictions were within ± 10% of observations for the three models ([Fig. 1](#page-4-2)). The means of normalized deviations (MND) were -0.022, 0.009, -0.049, and 0.006 for BH546, Melkassa-II, Dhidhesa, and Nassir cultivars, respectively ([Fig. 1](#page-4-2)). The MND closer to zero (-0.05 < MNR < 0.05) showed the models acceptably predicted observations for the different cropping systems tested ([Figs. 1 and 2](#page-4-2)).

Models performance evaluation based on the other indices are shown in [Table 4.](#page-6-2) The paired t-test showed no significant difference between simulated and observed values of all variables from the different experiments except for kernel wt. for the 0 and 100 kg ha−1 N rates (Experiment-II), and grain N, kernel wt. and LAI for Experiment-III. The correlation coefficient (r) was > 0.75 for 13 of the 16 paired comparisons made from all experiments. The magnitude of ME were small and associated with different signs that showed errors were not consistently directional. Bias were between 0.85 to 1.09 Mg ha−1 , indicating that model predictions were close to measured data. The dvalues between 0.93 and 0.98, the RMSE less or similar to the observed standard deviation ([Fig. 2](#page-5-0)) and the nRME values less than 15% for most of the variables from all datasets evaluated illustrated the models well predicted measured data under the different crop production situations in Ethiopia.

#### 3.2. Evaluation of generated weather datasets using observed weather

Generated daily rainfall from WeatherMan (r = 0.12), NASA (r = 0.34), MarkSim (r = 0.09) and GYGA (r = 0.33) were weakly correlated to observed daily rainfall ([Table 5](#page-6-3)). The ME of -0.89 mm for GYGA and -0.29 for NASA indicated over- and under-estimation, respectively, of daily rainfall but these datasets had higher R2 compared to MarkSim and WeatherMan ([Table 5)](#page-6-3). Except for GYGA (Bias = 0.77), the other three datasets had bias close to unity for daily rainfall. RMSE from all the evaluated datasets were < 10.5 mm for daily rainfall and the lowest (7 mm) and highest (10 mm) RMSE were from NASA and MarkSim datasets, respectively ([Table 5)](#page-6-3). NASA (EF = -0.10) and GYGA (EF = -0.39) datasets were better able to estimate observed daily rainfall time series compared to others ([Table 5)](#page-6-3). Comparing decadal rainfall, both NASA and GYGA datasets showed good agreement (RMSE < 30 mm; EF > 0.35) with observed data [(Table 5)](#page-6-3). However, relatively greater error was associated with GYGA [ME = -27 mm; intercept (b) = 26 mm] and NASA (ME = -9 mm; b = 25 mm), and these two datasets performed the best at estimating monthly rainfall total ([Table 5;](#page-6-3) [Fig. 3)](#page-7-0). WeatherMan was the best at estimating annual total rainfall [(Table 5)](#page-6-3).

NASA underestimated daily Tmax by 2.76 °C and overestimated daily Tmin by 2.28 °C ([Table 5)](#page-6-3). Except for NASA, the generated datasets had bias = 1.00 for Tmax ([Table 5)](#page-6-3). GYGA resulted in the lowest RMSE for both daily Tmax and Tmin. Further, time series observed daily Tmax and Tmin were relatively well-estimated with GYGA (EF = 0.71 for Tmax; EF = 0.38 for Tmin). The RMSE from all the evaluated datasets were < 5 °C for both daily Tmax and Tmin, with the lowest RMSE from GYGA dataset ([Table 5)](#page-6-3). Considering all indices determined, WeatherMan, followed by GYGA, performed well in simulating decadal, monthly and annual values of Tmax ([Table 5;](#page-6-3) [Fig. 4)](#page-8-0). The Tmin was best simulated by WeatherMan and MarkSim for all time periods ([Table 5;](#page-6-3) [Fig. 4)](#page-8-0), whereas NASA performed the worst in simulating Tmax and Tmin for the decadal, monthly and annual time periods ([Table 5;](#page-6-3) [Fig. 4)](#page-8-0).

#### 3.3. Combined weather dataset

The evaluation results for generated weather datasets generally showed good agreement between observed and GYGA rainfall and WeatherMan Tmax and Tmin for the daily and extended time periods. In addition, simulated grain yield with GYGA weather data showed the best agreement with simulations using observed weather data in this study. Hence, daily solar radiation and rainfall from GYGA dataset, and Tmax and Tmin from WeatherMan dataset were combined to develop a new weather dataset for use in running crop simulation models for sites that lack observed weather data in the complex topography of Ethiopia. This weather dataset is hereafter called "COMBINED" dataset. The COMBINED was evaluated by directly using in crop models to assess its performance and to compare with the other weather datasets.
