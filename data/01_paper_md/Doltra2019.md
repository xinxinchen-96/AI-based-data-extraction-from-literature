## 2. Materials and methods

### 2.1. Experimental data

Datasets from organic crop rotations covering short and long-term field experiments in different agro-climatic environments in Europe were used for testing and adjusting the FASSET model. The selection included seven datasets from six countries (Norway, Denmark, Poland, Switzerland, Italy and Spain), representing different climates, soils and fertility building measures. The fertility building measures include fertilization type, green manures, cover crops, tillage, crop rotation composition and management (organic, integrated or conventional). Collected experiments cover a variety of cropping systems and crop rotations, comprising field data and measures such as crop dry matter and N yield, soil mineral N and soil N2O emissions. Not all measurements were, however, available in all the experiments, with lesser data for soil N dynamics than for crop productivity in the study datasets. Provided data on soil mineral N included Foulum (Denmark), Frick (Switzerland), Osiny (Poland) and Værnes (Norway) experiments and data on N2O fluxes were taken at Foulum and Frick. Details of sites, soil texture, climate (annual precipitation and temperature), fertility measures and duration of the experiments are summarized in [Table 1](#page-2-0). Below we provide a brief summary for each site and references with more details in the experiments including measurements of crop yields or soil N variables.

Værnes (63°27′N, 10°57′E) is located in central Norway. The soil is sandy loam with 1.39% of soil organic carbon (SOC) and a C/N ratio of 13.2 at 0–20 cm depth ([Frøseth et al., 2014](#page-10-17)). At this location, the soil had not been farmed organically until the experiment started in 2008. Here the crop rotations were dominated by annual crops until 2000 and by grass from 2001 to 2006, while cereals were grown in 2007. The aim of the short-term experiment was to study the effect of various strategies for a grass-clover green manure ley on yield and N recovery of a subsequent spring barley crop. One treatment with mineral fertilizer as a control (only 80 kg N ha-1 ) (I) and four organic treatments combining the application of herbage-based digestate and grass-clover preceding spring barley (0 M-D, digestate and grass-clover pre-crop; D, digestate with oats as a pre-crop; 0 M, no digestate and grass-clover pre-crop; and 3 M no digestate with mulching of grass-clover) were investigated. More detailed information about the rotations, management and measurements is available in [Frøseth et al. (2014)](#page-10-17).

Foulum (56°30′N, 9°34′E) is located in Denmark. The soil is loamy sand with 2.3% SOC and a C/N ratio of 12.8 [(Chirinda et al., 2010](#page-9-6)). Three main cropping systems for cereal production were represented (O2 and O4 organic, C4 conventional), differing with regard to leguminous crops in the rotation, use of catch crops (CC), and application of animal manure or inorganic fertilizer (M). More detailed information about the rotations, management and measurements is provided in [Olesen et al. (2000)](#page-10-18); [Chirinda et al. (2010)](#page-9-6) and [Brozyna et al (2013)](#page-9-7).

Osiny (51°28′N, 22°03′E) is located in East-Central Poland. The soil is loamy sand and has a content of 0.85% SOC. The treatments included five different rotation types and associated management: organic, integrated, conventional intensive, conventional extensive and monoculture of winter wheat ([Król et al., 2013](#page-10-19); [Jonczyk et al., 2018](#page-10-20)).

Frick (47°30′N, 8°01′E) is located in North Switzerland. The soil is loamy clay with a 2.2% SOC. The treatments differ in tillage intensity (CT, conventional tillage at 15–18 cm, and RT, reduced tillage at 7–10 cm of depth) and fertilization system (S, cattle slurry, and MC, cattle manure compost in addition to reduced amounts of cattle slurry). The field had been managed organically since 1995. More detailed information can be found in [Berner et al. (2008)](#page-9-8) and [Krauss et al. (2017)](#page-10-21).

Montepaldi (43°40′N, 11°09′ E') is located in Tuscany, North West Italy. The soil texture is silty clay loam with 1.7% SOC and a C/N ratio of 7.1. Details of the long-term experiment can be found in [Lazzerini](#page-10-22) [et al. (2014)](#page-10-22) and [Migliorini et al. (2014)](#page-10-23). ### 2.2. FASSET model

The FASSET model ([www.fasset.dk)](http://www.fasset.dk) is a whole-farm model that simulates crop growth and soil C and N dynamics in the soil-crop-atmosphere system, including greenhouse gas fluxes, at a daily time scale. A detailed description of the crop model can be found in [Olesen et al.](#page-10-13) [(2002)](#page-10-13) and [Berntsen et al. (2003)](#page-9-3). All cropping systems were simulated with the latest version 2.5 of the FASSET model. Input data necessary to run the model comprised climate (at least daily values of maximum and minimum temperatures, precipitation and solar radiation), data related to soil physical-chemical characteristics (such as soil texture, bulk density and organic matter) and management information (sowing, harvesting, fertilization and tillage) of the aforementioned experiments.

Soil model initialization was done by simulating 2–4 years prior to the start of the treatment using the available management information at the plot level and historical weather data. When such historical management data were not available, the crop sequence under study was repeated in the simulation in order to initialize the model (i. e. sites in Spain). The influence of the level of available information for FASSET initialization of soil organic matter pools has been highlighted in other modelling studies ([Doltra et al., 2015](#page-10-15)), and with other dynamic crop models (e.g. [Luo et al., 2014](#page-10-25)).

### 2.3. Model parameterization and evaluation

The model was adjusted using data of DM and N yield and estimated maximum leaf area index (LAI) in treatments at all the experimental sites. In a first step, crop phenology parameters of the model were adjusted to reproduce the average crop development within a given site (based on observations, expert knowledge or the literature). With adjusted crop phenology parameters, simulated crop yield, biomass DM and N were evaluated. If necessary, crop parameters related to radiation use efficiency, canopy development and grain N storage were also adjusted in the model [(Table 2](#page-3-0)). Model behaviour was evaluated by comparing observed and simulated values of crop DM and N yield, soil mineral N and N2O fluxes.

#### 2.4. Statistical analysis

Five statistical indices were calculated to explore and assess model ability to simulate both crop productivity and soil N variables. First, the coefficient of determination r 2 , which is an indicator of the closeness of the relationship between model predictions and observations, was used:

$$r^2 = \frac{\left(\sum_{l} (\mathbf{M}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{M}})(\mathbf{O}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{O}})\right)^2}{\sum (\mathbf{M}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{M}})^2 \sum (\mathbf{O}_l - \overset{\cdot}{\mathbf{O}})^2}$$

where Oi and Mi are the observed and modelled values; *O* ¯ and *M* ¯ are the means of all the observed and modelled values. This parameter ranges from 0 to 1, with values close to 1 indicating that the model is highly precise, while the lowest precision level is represented by 0.

Second, the index of agreement (d), which is a dimensionless statistical index varying between 0 and 1. It reflects the relative size of the differences between model simulations and observations ([Willmott,](#page-10-26) [1982](#page-10-26)):

$$\mathbf{d} = \mathbf{1} - \frac{\sum_{l}^{n} \left( \mathbf{O}_{l} - \mathbf{M}_{l} \right)^{2}}{\sum_{l}^{n} \left( l O_{l}^{\prime} l - l \mathbf{M}^{\prime} l \right)^{2}}$$

where *ǀO*′ *ǀi* and *ǀM*′ *ǀ i* are the differences with respect to the average observation, of the observed and model simulated values, respectively.

Third, the root mean square error (RMSE), which is a measure of the differences between observations and predictions, was calculated following the equation proposed by [Willmott (1982)](#page-10-26):

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{l}^{n} (O_l - M_l)^2}{n}}$$

where n is the number of observations. The lower the RMSE, the better the simulated values agree with the observations.

Fourth, the mean bias error (MBE) shows the systematic deviations. A negative MBE is an indication of model underestimation and a positive value indicates overestimation. This statistical index was calculated by the equation of [Willmott (1982)](#page-10-26):

$$\text{MBE} = \frac{\sum_{l}^{n} \left( \mathbf{M}_{l} - \mathbf{O}_{l} \right)}{\mathbf{n}}$$

Finally, the model efficiency (EF) was calculated following [Nash and](#page-10-27)

### Sutcliff[e (1970)](#page-10-27):

$$\text{EF} = 1 - \frac{\sum (\mathbf{M}_{\parallel} - \mathbf{O}_{l})^{2}}{\sum (\mathbf{O}_{l} - \mathbf{O})^{2}}$$

EF ranges from −1 to 1, with an efficiency of 1 indicating the perfect match of the simulations to the observations, while values lower than zero indicate that the mean of the observations is a better predictor than the simulated value.
