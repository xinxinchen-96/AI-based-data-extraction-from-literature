#### 2.2. Field layout and experimental details

Experiments were conducted during the Rabi (post-monsoon winter) season for the wheat crop during the years 2002–2003, 2003–2004 and 2004–2005. During the wheat crop experiment, irrigation treatment based on scheduling criteria and fertilizer treatment comprising different levels of fertilizer application were adopted. The irrigation treatments consisted of irrigation scheduling based on maximum allowable depletion (MAD) of available soil water (ASW) criteria, which is given as

- I1 = 10% MAD of ASW;
- I2 = 40% MAD of ASW;
- I3 = 60% MAD of ASW.

The fertilizer treatments adopted for wheat crop were as given below:

- F1 = control treatment with application of N:P2O5:K2O as 0:0:0 kg ha-1 ;

The major purpose of the crop experiments was to generate observed data over a wider range for the benefit of simulation. Therefore the irrigation was scheduled over a wide range (10– 60%) of maximum allowable depletion of available soil water. Similarly the level of fertilizer application varied between no fertilization to almost double the normal rate of application for the winter wheat crop in the region. The recommended fertilizer dose for the wheat crop of the study area is N:P2O5:K2O as 80:40:40 kg ha-1 . The recommended fertilizer dose has been decided on the basis of nutrient status (obtained through soil testing) of the experimental area.

The experimental area was divided into 36 plots of 5 m 4 m size maintaining a buffer of 1 m between adjacent plots. The field experiments were designed as per Completely Randomized Block Design (CRBD) with treatments as the factors. There were three irrigation treatments and four fertilizer treatments including one control treatment. There were three replications for each treatment. A seed rate of 100 kg ha-1 was used for wheat crop. The seed was sown at a row spacing of 20 cm and a plant spacing of 5 cm during all the three experiments. This gave a plant density of 106 plants per hectare. N, P and K fertilizers were applied in the form of urea, single super phosphate (SSP) and muriate of potash (MOP) respectively. Fertilizer N was broadcasted to soil in two equal splits, at the time of sowing and 30 days after sowing. The phosphatic and potassic fertilizers were basally applied at the time of land preparation for wheat crop by broadcasting and mixed manually with top 7.5 cm soil.

### 2.3. Irrigation scheduling

Irrigation scheduling was based on the percentage depletion of available soil water in the root zone. Available soil water was considered as the difference between root zone water storage at field capacity and permanent wilting point. For estimating available soil water storage, the irrigation management depth of the wheat crop was assumed as 60 cm, irrespective of the growth stage ([Panda et al., 2003)](#page-8-0). The maximum allowable depletions (MAD) of the available soil water were fixed at 10, 40 and 60%. In order to assess the changes in soil water status, soil moisture was measured in 0–15, 15–30, 30–45, 45–60, and 60– 90 cm layers of soil profile. In top 0–15 cm layer where the neutron probe (Hydroprobe) could not be used, moisture content was measured gravimetrically. An access tube made of seamless aluminum alloy (50 cm internal diameter) was installed in the center of each experimental plot for soil moisture measurements using the neutron probe. The time changes of soil moisture at 15– 30, 30–45, 45–60, and 60–90 cm soil layers were estimated with the help of Hydroprobe (Model 503 of Campbell Pacific Nuclear Corporation, U.S.A.). Calibration curves showing the relationship between the neutron probe count ratio and volumetric soil water content were prepared for the experimental soil profile prior to the start of the experiments. Using the temporal data of soil moisture measured by a neutron probe and gravimetric measurements, the percentage depletion of available soil water in the effective root zone was estimated by the following equation ([Martin et al., 1990](#page-8-0)):

maximum allowable depletion ð%Þ

$$= 100 \times \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i}^{n} \frac{F\mathbf{C}_{i} - \theta_{i}}{F\mathbf{C}_{i} - \text{PWP}} \tag{2.1}$$

where n is the number of layers in the effective rooting depth used for the soil moisture sampling, FCi is the soil moisture at field capacity in the ith layer, ui is the soil moisture in ith layer before irrigation and PWP is the soil moisture at permanent wilting point. The amount of water applied after the attainment of predefined MAD was calculated as

$$V_d = \frac{\mathbf{MAD}(\%) \times (\mathbf{FC} - \mathbf{PWP}) \times \mathbf{R}_{\mathbb{Z}} \times \mathbf{A}}{100} \tag{2.2}$$

where Vd is the volume of irrigation water (m3 ), Rz is the effective rooting depth (m) and A is the surface area of the plot (m2 ). The surface area of each plot was 20 m2 while the effective root zone depth was assumed as 60 cm for all treatments. Measured volume of irrigation water was applied to each plot with the help of a hosepipe and a water meter. The water meter was calibrated and tested for accuracy before its use. Water was introduced at one end and was allowed to rapidly spread over the entire basin. Equal amount of irrigation was applied for each treatment up to 30 days after sowing. Different irrigation treatments were maintained 30 days after sowing by applying different volumes of irrigation water at different intervals under different irrigation regimes. For example, in case of I1 irrigation treatment, 10 numbers of irrigations amounting to 12 mm of water was applied at 4–5 days interval. In case of I2 irrigation treatment, 2 numbers of irrigations amounting to 48 mm was applied at 20–25 days interval whereas in case of I3 irrigation treatment, single irrigation amounting 72 mm was applied at 30 days interval during all the crop experiments.

#### 2.4. Growth parameters

In order to evaluate the effect of different levels of fertilizer application and moisture regimes on crop responses, data on the growth attributes of wheat crop were collected. Postharvest data pertaining to the straw yield and grain yield were measured on the day before general harvest of wheat crop. These data were necessary for estimation of water use efficiency and for evaluating the effects of different treatments on the final crop yield. The growth parameters, such as plant height, number of branches and leaves, and leaf area index (LAI) were recorded at an interval of 15 days after the establishment of the crop. Two representative plants were harvested from each treatment plot for determination of the above growth parameters. The maximum leaf area index (LAI) was calculated from the LAI data collected at different growth stages.

#### 2.5. Crop water use (WU) and water use efficiency (WUE)

Crop water used (WU) by the crop was estimated from the depletion of soil water storage in the root zone taking into account rainfall, irrigation and deep percolation, and computing the water balance as [(Sahoo, 2004)](#page-8-0):

$$\mathbf{W}\mathbf{U} = \sum_{t_1}^{t_2} \Delta \mathbf{s} \Delta \mathbf{t} + (\mathbf{R} + I) - DP \tag{2.3}$$

where WU is the crop water use in cm, Ds is the change in soil water storage in the root zone in cm, R is the rainfall in cm, I is irrigation applied in cm, DP is the deep percolation loss in cm and t1 and t2 are the start and end of the time interval under consideration.

Water use efficiency (WUE) was estimated as

$$\text{WUE} = \frac{Y}{\text{WU}} \text{ kg ha}^{-1} \text{cm}^{-1} \tag{2.4}$$

where Y was grain yield in kg ha-1 .

#### 2.6. Simulation of crop growth

DSSAT (Decision Support System for Agrotechnology Transfer) was used for simulating crop growth parameters ([Hoogenboom](#page-7-0) [et al., 1994](#page-7-0)). DSSAT is an integrated software of different computer programmes, which facilitate the application of crop simulation models in research and decision-making. For the present study CERES-wheat model from DSSAT v4.0 was used for the wheat crop. In CERES model, the input and output files carry data related to soil, weather, genotype and crop. The files structure is described in three different categories: input files, output files and experimental data files. Input files are further divided into those dealing with the experiment, weather and soil, and the characteristics of different genotypes (crop and cultivar).

#### 2.6.1. Experimental detail file

This input file structure has maximum flexibility but it requires minimum data set to run the simulation. The structure is designed to vary input for a variety of crop, soil, fertilizer level, and weather for same experimental file. The file heading contains the experiment code and name, the treatment combinations, and details of the experimental conditions (field characteristics, soil analysis data, initial soil water and inorganic nitrogen conditions, seedbed preparation and planting geometries, irrigation and water management, fertilizer management, organic residue applications, chemical applications, tillage operations, environmental modifications, harvest management), and simulation controls. In experimental input file, there is a separate simulation section, which was used to simulate and run the crop model under the situation already mentioned in input files and to get the output results in the form of growth and yield of crops.

#### 2.6.2. Weather data file

Daily weather data at least from planting date to harvest date was loaded for each crop growth period separately to run a particular crop file. The minimum weather data, such as daily solar radiation, maximum, minimum temperature and rainfall were included. This minimum data set of weather parameters was used in the calibration and validation of CERES-wheat. Solar radiation was estimated from bright sunshine hours using the standard Angstrom formula [(Angstrom, 1924](#page-7-0)). The other required informations, such as name of the country, latitude, longitude, elevation, etc. were also used to create the weather data file for the station of Indian Institute of Technology, Kharagpur.

#### 2.6.3. Soil data file

The data related to soil profile, soil water, soil nitrogen and root growth characteristics, soil taxonomic classification, soil texture and other descriptive data of the experimental site were used to develop the soil file for the experimental station. The file generally contains information that is available for the soil at a particular experimental site, and supplementary information extracted from a soil survey database for a soil of the same taxonomic classification as the soil at the experimental site. The data in the soils file are arranged so that entries need to be made only for the aspects simulated.

#### 2.6.4. Genotype

The data related to morphological and physiological characteristics of a crop are used to create the genotype input file. This file was used by the model to create several genetic coefficients, which described how the genotype of a specific crop variety was influenced by the environmental factors, such as maximum and minimum temperatures, day length, etc. Genetic coefficients of CERES-wheat are related to photoperiod sensitivity, duration of grain filling, conversion of mass to grain number, grain filling rates, vernalization requirement, stem size and cold hardiness.

#### 2.6.5. Output files

There are standard output files created for each simulation run with two main files namely, OVERVIEW.OUT and SUMMARY.OUT. The outputs of each experiment were stored in the overview file, which displays all the options of the input file, crop performance and simulation results. The OVERVIEW file was checked after each run to view the information related to description of input files, crop status at different growth stages, display of simulated variables and stress factors in major developmental phases of the crop. All the output files were stored as SUMMARY.OUT file for each crop. The summary files were used and appended when the simulation was run during the next time. The output files can include additional information, which may relate to parameter changes for sensitivity analysis.
