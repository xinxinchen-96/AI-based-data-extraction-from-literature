## 2. Materials and methods

#### 2.1. Experimental site and materials

The study was conducted over a period of 4 years from April 2013 to October 2016 at the experimental farm located in Jinxiang, Shandong province, China (34°58′42″N, 116°10′56″E). This region has the monsoon climate of medium latitudes, with an annual average temperature of 14.0∘ C. There is an annual precipitation of 600–800 mm, a mild winter climate and a relatively humid, hot and rainy summer, with rainfall concentrated in July and August. As a local conventional cropping system, cultivation of summer cotton, winter garlic had been rotated since 1980s' at the site. The soil was Typic Ochri-Aquic Cambosols in the Chinese soil taxonomy and was Inceptisols in American soil taxonomy ([Gong et al., 2007](#page-8-14)). The selected basic soil physical and chemical properties of the 0–20 and 20–40 cm soil before planting were as follows ([Table 1)](#page-1-0).

The conventional fertilizers used were urea (containing 46% N) and diammonium phosphate (DAP, containing 48% P2O5 and 18% N) as N fertilizer, DAP as P fertilizer, and potassium sulfate (containing 60% K2O) as K fertilizer, respectively. The epoxy resin coating of sulfurcoated urea (PSCU, N 38%, coating thickness 17.5%, the longevity was 2 months) and epoxy resin-coated urea (PCU, N 42%, coating thickness 4.0%, the longevity was 3 months) freely provided by Kingenta Ecological Engineering Co., Ltd., China were used as CRU. The longevity of PCU and PSCU in 25 °C water was 70 and 90 days, respectively, according to "State Standard of the People's Republic of China-Slow Release Fertilizer" [(Liu et al., 2009)](#page-8-15). The same types of product were used throughout 4 years. The cultivar used in the present study was "Lu Yanmian 28" for cotton and "Jinxiang White skin" for garlic. They are currently major cultivars widely cultivated under the cotton-garlic intercropping system in North China plain.

### 2.2. Experimental design and field managements

The experiment was designed as a completely randomized block with three replicates and seven fertilizer treatments: CRU50% (100 kg ha−1 ), CRU100% (200 kg ha−1 ), CRU150% (300 kg ha−1 ), Urea50% (100 kg ha−1 ), Urea100% (200 kg ha−1 ), Urea150% (300 kg ha−1 ) and CK (a control without fertilizer). The N rates tested were 0, 100, 200 and 300 kg N ha−1 in both cotton and garlic season. Both the urea and CRU were single applied once as per usual on-farm practice. 90 kg ha−1 P2O5 and 180 kg ha−1 K2O at each season were basally applied in all fertilization treatments. In CRU treatments, 50% of the total N was applied by PCU and PSCU at equal proportion and another part was provided by urea and DAP. The N source in urea treatments were urea and DAP.

Plots were arranged randomly with an area of 22 m2 (4.4 m × 5 m). The garlic was sowed in mid-October with a density of 75,000 plants ha−1 , and harvested in early June, while cotton was soil bowl seedling in mid-April, transplanted garlic row space in early May with a density of 20,800 plants ha−1 , fertilized in mid-June and harvested in early October (Fig.S1). There was about 30 coexisted days for the two crops growing together in field. After garlic harvested, the chemical fertilizers for cotton were deep-strip till applied in soil at a depth of 15–20 cm. Meanwhile, the tillage practice for garlic was spread on the surface, and then plowed to a depth of over 15 cm before sowing. All the agronomic practices were applied equally for all plants as per usual on-farm practice.

#### 2.3. Sampling and measurement

The N content and longevity of CRU (PCU and PSCU) in water were determined by the method of "State Standard of the People's Republic of China-Slow Release Fertilizer" [(Liu et al., 2009](#page-8-15)). The N release characteristics of CRU in water showed in Fig.S2. Under field conditions, the N cumulative release rates were measured by a weight loss method ([Wilson et al., 2009; Gao et al., 2015)](#page-9-8). Briefly, 42 mesh bags each containing 10 g PCU or PSCU particles were buried before cotton fertilization and 60 bags before garlic fertilization. Three bags were randomly collected 10, 20, 30, 50, 70, 90 and 110 d after buried in cotton season in 2015 and collected 10, 20, 30, 60, 90, 120, 150, 180, 200 and 220 d after buried in garlic season in 2015–2016. The N release rates were determined as [Geng et al. (2016)](#page-8-13) ([Fig. 1)](#page-2-0). As reported previously ([Hara, 2000)](#page-8-16), the Richards function enables the calculation of

At the maturity, the garlic bulb yields were measured by hand from 10 m2 in the center of each plot. Seed cotton yield were measured by the arithmetic product of boll weight from 100 open bolls and bolls from 20 consecutive plants each plot. Before cotton uprooted, 100 open bolls ( > 2 cm in diameter) was randomly picked and air dried to a constant weight and then calculated the boll weight, measured the lint percentage. 20 consecutive plants in the middle two rows were used to survey bolls and stem diameter. The soil samples were taken at 20 cm intervals from 0 to 100 cm depth, after the harvest of cotton in 2013, 2014, 2015 and 2016, respectively. Three soil cores, one from the fertilization strip, one behind cotton plants and one between cotton lines, were collected from the 0–20, 20–40, 40–60, 60–80 and 80–100 cm soil layers, respectively. Fresh soil samples of the same soil depth per plot were mixed as a composite sample throughout the whole plots. Soil samples were divided into two parts. One part was stored fresh in a 4 ° C refrigerator, and another part was air-dried, sieved through a 2.0 mm and a 0.25 mm screen, respectively, and stored for further analysis.

The concentrations of NO3 −-N and NH4 +-N (extraction with 0.01 M CaCl2) in fresh soil samples were analyzed in extract solution using the AA3-A001-02E Auto-analyzer (Bran-Luebbe, Germany) within 48 h after the collection. Soil organic matter was determined by using a wet oxidation method with K2Cr2O7 and concentrated H2SO4·Total N content was measured by using a Kjeldahl digestion method. Soil pH value was probed at a 1:2.5 ratio of soil to CO2-free distilled water using a pH meter (PB-10, Sartorius AG, Göttingen, Germany). Soil available P content was determined using the Olsen-P method, based on extraction of air-dry soil with 0.5 M NaHCO3 at pH 8.5. Soil available K content was measured by using the CH3COONH4 extraction method and detected by a flame photometer. The cationic exchangeable capacity (CEC) was measured by the procedure of [Thomas et al. (2007).](#page-9-9)

# 2.4. Data statistical analysis

All results are presented as means of treatments. Analysis of variance and mean separation tests (Duncan's multiple range test and least significant difference test [LSD]) were performed using Statistical Analysis System (SAS) package version 9.2 (2010, SAS Institute, Cary, NC). In the regression analysis, regression coefficients were calculated and their significant was test through general statistical procedure (2010, SAS Institute, Cary, NC).

# 3. Results

# 3.1. N release characteristics of PCU and PSCU under field conditions

The N cumulative release curve of the PSCU under cotton field conditions was inverted "L" shaped, which indicated an initial 40 d quick release stage, ended with an reduced N release stage (40–120d) ([Fig. 1](#page-2-0)a). The pattern of temporal release from PCU was linear release. There were almost 94.2% and 98.6% of N was released when cotton uprooted. The N release curve of PCU and PSCU in water extraction (Fig.S1) had a similar release trend with in soil condition for the similar parameters ([Table 2](#page-2-1)). This was probably because the air temperature during cotton growth (May to October) was generally the same with 25 °C water ([Fig. 2](#page-3-0)a).

The PCU and PSCU revealed a quick release stage in the first 30th days, and followed by slow release stage from 30th to 150th days (22.4% and 17.8% was released from PCU and PSCU, respectively), and the N release rate accelerated from bolting stage in the garlic field ([Fig. 1](#page-2-0)b). More than 94.7% and 96.3% of total N released from PCU and PSCU after garlic bulb harvested, respectively. The vestigial excess N was resealed in soil when it was needed for next crop. Differences in the N release rates of the CRU during cotton and garlic growth probably because the temperature during cotton growth was higher than garlic ([Fig. 2](#page-3-0)b).

#### 3.2. Cotton plant growth and yields as affected by CRU

Four years data showed that CRU outperformed granular urea in increasing cotton yields ([Table 3](#page-3-1)). CRU treatments achieved higher lint cotton yields by 10.1%–11.6%, 7.0%–11.5%, 5.4%–7.3% and 5.2%–17.3% in 2013, 2014, 2015 and 2016, respectively, compared with urea treatment. Although no significant difference found between CRU100% and Urea150%, the lint cotton yields of Urea150% achieved significantly higher than Urea100% (except for 2016). Lint cotton yield of CRU100% was showed no significant with CRU150% treatment except for 2014, suggesting that 200 kg N ha−1 is possible with CRU while maintaining the higher yield and also fertilizer-saving. #### 3.4. Soil NO3 −-N and NH4 +-N contents as affected by CRU

The content of soil NO3 −-N was concentrated in 0–20 cm profile, and it increased with the increase of N rates both in urea and CRU treatments ([Fig. 4)](#page-5-0). In 2013, the contents of NO3 ‐ -N in CRU50% treatment were significantly higher than Urea50% treatment of 20–40 cm soil, but no difference was found between CRU and urea treatments in 40–100 cm soil. In 2014, the NO3 −-N content of CRU treatments was higher at 0–20 cm soil layers than urea treatments, but it was significantly lower in CRU150% than in Urea150% in 40–80 cm soil. In 2015 and 2016, soil NO3 −-N contents were decreased in deeper soil layers, while it was increased in 60–80 cm soil of Urea100% and Urea150% treatments. The NO3 −-N content of CRU treatments was increased at 0–20 cm soil layer compared with urea treatments, but the opposite trend was found in 60–100 cm. Meanwhile, the highest NO3 −- N content at 60–80 cm occurred in Urea150%, followed in descending order by CRU100%, Urea50%, and CRU50%. This indicated that NO3 −- N had leached to the 60–80 cm soil and CRU decreased NO3 −-N leaching to deep soil layer.

#### 3.5. Soil organic matter and soil total N contents

The organic matter content was increased (except CK and Urea50%) compared with the initial value (15.06 g kg−1 ) in 0–20 cm soil and it decreased with soil depth ([Table 5)](#page-6-0). Soil organic matter content increased with the increase of N application rates both in urea and CRU sources in 0–20 cm, no difference was found among all treatments in 60–100 cm soil. Soil organic matter content of CRU50% and CRU100% was significantly higher than Urea50% and Urea100%, respectively. No significant difference was observed among all treatments in 40–100 cm soil layers, which was about 7.17–9.34 g kg−1 in soil below 60 cm.

Soil total N content decreased with increasing depth in the 0–100 cm profile ([Table 5)](#page-6-0). Total N content in 0–20 cm soil profile increased with increasing rates of N application both in urea and CRU treatments. The total N content of CRU treatments was higher at 0–20 cm soil layer than urea treatments in all the four years. However, the differences of soil total N were not significant among CRU and urea treatments in the 40–100 cm soil layers.

In this study, four years' data showed that lint cotton and garlic yields of CRU were increased by 7.0%–17.2% and 2.3%–7.7%, respectively, relative to urea treatments ([Table 3](#page-3-1) and [Table 4](#page-4-1)). Meanwhile, number of bolls and signal boll weight were increased by using CRU while lint percentage was not affected by the type of N fertilizer. CRU increased cotton yield by improved boll numbers while [Geng et al. (2015b)](#page-8-11) reported that CRU increased the boll weight compared to urea. In addition, the lint cotton yield showed significantly positive correlations with soil NO3 −-N content, soil organic matter and total N contents ([Table 8)](#page-7-1), implying that cotton yield was complexly influenced by multiple factors either in combination or alone. Both the cotton and garlic yield of Urea150% was significantly increased compared with Urea100% (except bulb yield in 2014 and cotton yield in 2016), but no significant difference found between CRU100% and Urea150% treatments. Hence, the CRU100% (200 kg N ha−1 ) reduced N rate by onethird achieved the same yield as with the 150% rate (300 kg N ha−1 ) of urea and CRU.

Under cotton-garlic intercropping system, cotton seedlings were seeded without fertilization and transplanted into the field before garlic bulb harvested. After about 20 days coexist, cotton was fertilized at the initial-squaring stage. Although there were little N demand during early cotton growth period [(Dong et al., 2010)](#page-8-0), the lack of N supply undoubtedly mitigated cotton growth. In this study, the accelerated N release rate at the garlic harvested stage, when temperature increased from April to June ([Fig. 2b](#page-3-0)), also enhanced cotton growth before fertilization. The peak N uptake stage of cotton was occurred from full bloom stage to full boll setting stage ([Hu et al., 2011)](#page-8-9). In this study, the N released from PSCU was quick in July for the increased temperature and rainfall when the cotton plant was relatively sensitive and urgent for N nutrient. Meanwhile, the liner release shape of PCU ([Fig. 1](#page-2-0)a) maintained cotton growth when it requires a lot of N for reproductive and vegetative growth in the later growth period.

Nutrient release rates of CRU are known to be significantly affected by temperature (Fujinuma et al., 2009; [Yang et al., 2011](#page-9-4)) and coating materials ([Yang et al., 2012](#page-9-7)). In this study, the PCU and PSCU was almost not released from 40 to 120 d after being buried in soil during the winter season [(Fig. 1](#page-2-0)a) when little N needed during garlic season was in the overwintering stage. The reason was that the soil temperature was below 0∘ C, and the CRU was almost not released in soil. The release rate was quick from bulb expansion stage to maturing stage when the temperature increased over 20∘ C ([Fig. 2b](#page-3-0)), which was according to a release peak stage of CRU (150–220 d). As a result, the CRU treatments achieved significantly higher garlic bulb yields. In summary, the release characteristic of CRU in natural field conditions was closely matched the demand for N during the whole growth period of crop under cotton-garlic intercropping system.

#### 4.2. Soil fertility and potential N leaching as affected by CRU

In China, the increase of chemical fertilizer input, especially N fertilizer, is a common practice for increasing crops yield ([Dong et al.,](#page-8-0) [2010; Xu et al., 2013)](#page-8-0). However, excessive N application amounts and inappropriate application methods lead to high N losses through leaching approach ([Yang et al., 2012; Zheng et al., 2016)](#page-9-7). In this study, the contents of NO3 −-N and NH4 +-N in 0–20 cm soil layer increased with higher N application both in urea and CRU treatments. In upland soil, significant soil NO3 −-N contents and enough rainfall or irrigation enhanced soil nitrate leaching [(Liang et al., 2011](#page-8-18)). As for cotton and garlic, the crop roots mainly occupied in the 0–40 cm soil, so excessive NO3 −-N contents below 40 cm soil not only decreased N use efficiency but also increased the risk of environmental pollution ([Shang et al.,](#page-8-19) [2014)](#page-8-19). Relative researches revealed that decreasing fertilization splits from two to one decreased NO3 −-N leaching by 91.2% of the initial N rate ([Zhang et al., 2015](#page-9-10)). In this study, the CRU treatments exhibited higher NO3 −-N contents in 0–40 cm soil relative to urea treatments, but the NO3 −-N content in urea treatment was higher in the 60–100 cm soil layer, especially after four years fertilization ([Fig. 4](#page-5-0)). The reason is that the NO3 −-N with a negative charge, so the NO3 −-N adsorption capacity of soil was smaller, resulted in that increased NO3 −-N contents and irrigation increased the leaching loss of NO3 −-N ([Zheng et al., 2016](#page-9-2)).

N fertilizer application significantly affected soil physical, chemical properties and microbial populations, all of which inevitably affected soil N cycling ([Tellez-Rio et al., 2015)](#page-9-11). In this study, the organic matter and total N contents increased with increasing N rate, but insufficient N supply decreased organic matter and total N contents ([Table 5)](#page-6-0). Similar results also proved by [Lu et al. (2009)](#page-8-20) that the relationship between soil C contents and N rates was linear shape, while insufficient N supply could cause consistent decreases in rice yields overtime ([Bi et al., 2009](#page-8-21)). It has been well demonstrated that urea application often reduces soil pH, because H+ is released from the roots during NH4 + uptake ([Marschner, 1995)](#page-8-22). Meanwhile, the NO3 −-N leach and unbalanced anions absorber with long-term application of N fertilizers also could result in soil acidification [(Schroder et al., 2011)](#page-8-23). In this study, the pH values of 0–20 cm soil in Urea150% and CRU150% treatments were lower than Urea50% and CRU50% treatments after four years fertilization ([Table 6)](#page-6-1). In this study, the available K content of CRU and urea treatments decreased with increased N application rates in 0–20 cm soil but no difference was observed between urea and CRU treatments in the same soil layer ([Table 7)](#page-6-2). #### 5. Conclusions

The successive release of N from CRU permitted the meeting of the full N requirements of crops grown under cotton-garlic intercropping system. Four years data showed that the cotton and garlic yields of CRU treatments were increased by 5.4%-17.3% and 2.0%-7.4%, respectively, compared with urea treatments. Although the CRU treatment at 200 kg N ha−1 had one-third less supplied N than that of conventional urea treatment (300 kg N ha−1 ), the cotton and garlic with CRU at 200 kg N ha−1 produced the same or more yields. Soil NO3 −-N content in CRU treatments was higher in 0–40 cm soil layer, but lower in 60–100 cm soil, especially after four years fertilization, indicating that CRU effectively reduced the leaching of NO3 −-N to deep soil layers. Contents of soil organic matter and total N in CRU treatments were higher than urea in 0–40 cm soil. No significant difference of soil pH, available P and available K contents between urea and CRU in the same soil layer was observed. Consequently, using moderate amounts (200 kg N ha−1 ) of CRU had great potential to increase cotton-garlic yields, reduced fertilizer rate, improved soil fertility, and decreased the leaching of NO3 −-N to deep soil layer in North China plain.
