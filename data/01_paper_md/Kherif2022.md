## *2.1. Climate and soil characteristics*

Data was collected from a field experiment carried out at an agronomic station in the Oued Smar region, northeast of Algiers (Algeria) (36◦42′ N, 3◦09′ E). This experiment was performed during two consecutive growing seasons (2018–2019 and 2019–2020). The climate can be described as Mediterranean sub-humid with an average yearly rainfall of 603 mm recorded during the last forty years. During this period, the annual temperature varied between 18.1 and 24.1 ◦C with an annual mean of 18.1 ◦C. (Table 1). This climate is also characterized by irregular rainfall in terms of frequency and quantity with long drought periods. The driest period interrupted only the second cropping season from the beginning to the end of March. There was a high contrast between the two growing seasons, in terms of rainfall and temperature. As that, the first cropping season (December. 2018 – June 2019) being wetter (total cumulated rainfall = 271 mm) and colder (average temperature = 14.2 ◦C) then the second cropping season (December. 2019 – June 2020) with cumulative rainfall of 189 mm and air temperature of 16.4 ◦C. Different soil characteristics such as sand, silt, and clay particules, and calcium carbonate (CaCO3) content, bulk density, and pH were measured at different soil depths (Table 1). Soil presented a silty clay loam texture at most of layers analyzed (from 0 to 100 cm). The soil of field experiment site was Verstisol, and presented quite homogenous conditions with no slope. The root growth is not restrained as the soil is deep and no physical constraints have been observed. The soil can be described as alkaline and non-calcareous, with deficiency for both available N and P. Soil parameters linked to physical and hydraulic characteristics were also measured as required for the STICS model such as bulk density and water content at permanent wilting point and field capacity (Table 1).

# *2.3. Plant and soil samplings and measurements*

Development stages for both crops were recorded with a particular attention to emergence (BBCH = 09) and flowering (BBCH = 65) dates. Initial soil samples were taken at the sowing date of each growing season which served for soil characterization and to measure initial soil water and mineral N (N-NH4 ++NO3 - ) content. Plant and soil samples were taken over five sampling periods during the first growing season. Three samplings were performed during the vegetative phase, one at the chickpea flowering stage (110 DAS), and the last sampling was done at harvest. In the second growing season three samplings periods were carried out, one during the vegetative phase, one at the flowering stage (107 DAS), while the last was done at harvest. At each sampling different variables of interest were measured such as: root depth, soil water and N content (at 0–20, 20–40, 40–60 and 60–80 cm), LAI, aboveground dry and wet biomass and N uptake by plant. At harvest grain yield, its components and the maximum plant height were measured. Soil moisture (at each 10 cm depth) was regularly measured by a soil probe (Diviner 2000, Sentek Pty Ltd, United States of America) for all plots with one observation per plot on a daily basis. The collected soil moisture values (in scaled frequency unit) from the probe measurements were calibrated (i.e. volumetric soil water content in m3 m-3 unit) according to methods reported by [Groves and Rose (2004)](#page-14-0) and [Haberland](#page-14-0)  [et al. (2014)](#page-14-0).

Physico-chemical analyses of the soil were performed through standard methods. Soil and plant N content were determined using Kjeldahl method ([Lynch and Barbano, 1999)](#page-15-0). Soil P content was measured with the Malachite green method after mixed digestion by both perchloric and nitric acids ([Valizadeh et al., 2003](#page-15-0)). The Anne Method [(McBratney](#page-15-0) [et al., 2000](#page-15-0)) was used to measure soil organic matter content. The Horton and Nelson method ([Leo, 1963)](#page-15-0) was performed to determine soil calcium carbonate content, while soil pH was measured in the soil suspension with deionized water (soil:water ratio = 1:2.5) with a pH meter ([Shen et al., 1996)](#page-15-0). The N acquired by plant in t ha-1 was estimated by multiplying dry biomass by the N concentration in the plant and subtracting initial seed N content from the whole biomass and grain N content. Climatic data were provided by the Dar Beida meteorological station (36◦72′ N, 3◦25′ E) which is located at 5.5 km from the experimental site.

#### *2.4. STICS model overview*

STICS is a daily time-step crop model in which phenology is mainly driven by thermal time ([Brisson et al., 2003)](#page-14-0). The plant is characterized by its shoot biomass and leaf area index ([Brisson et al., 2008](#page-14-0)). STICS simulates dynamically the establishment of the canopy, the biomass production and root development (including N and water absorption) ([Falconnier et al., 2019)](#page-14-0). Yield formation is decomposed into a grain setting process, which computes the number of produced grains, and a grain-filling process. These processes define the simulation of dry matter and N accumulated in the grain. The model also simulates the effects of N, water, and thermal stresses as well as anoxia. Intercropping systems can be modeled by STICS using a simple representation of the system divided into the dominant canopy and the understory canopy which is divided into a shaded and a sunlit part. Each canopy is defined by a light microclimate estimated by a radiative balance which drives the different dynamics of the three sub-systems ([Brisson et al., 2004)](#page-14-0). The model proposes two options for simulating radiation interception: (i) a direct application of the Beer's law, which is used for homogenous crops, and a radiation transfer approach, which is more suited for crop in rows (e.g. intercropping systems). For the crop water requirement, the model proposes a crop coefficient approach for which the potential evapotranspiration is defined as a driving variable. However, this approach is not suitable when the Beer's law could not be applied directly (i.e. use of the radiative transfer formalism). The alternative is a resistive approach which estimates plant water requirements and soil evaporation using the Shuttleworth and Wallace daily time-step model ([Brisson et al., 1998a](#page-14-0)).

## *2.5. Model inputs and simulation options*

STICS requires as inputs, soil and climate data, in addition to crop management and plant parameters. We set most of the soil parameters directly from the results of soil analysis [(Table 1)](#page-2-0), and/or by direct calculation in the case of some soil parameters (i.e. pedo-transfer function). We also parameterized both initial water and N content of the soil. Climate inputs include minimum and maximum air temperature, global radiation, rainfall, wind and relative humidity. For each combination of growing season (i.e. 2018/2019 and 2019/2020) × cropping system (i.e. sole wheat, sole chickpea, intercropped wheat and chickpea) we parameterized a crop management file (i.e. plant density, dates and depth of sowing and also the respective dates of N-application and irrigation). We considered a depth of 80 cm for the calculation of soil N and water stocks. This could lead to well calibration for other parameters (e.g. rooting depth). The harvest date was simulated based on seed water content. The emergence density was used as model input instead of sowing density in order to have a more realistic simulation. This last precaution was adopted to better take in consideration the impact of other factors (e.g. biotic stresses) that are not simulated by the model. Regardless the use of radiative transfer formalism during intercroppingsimulation, the inter-row width and the row orientation was parameterized. All treatments (combinations of cropping systems and N fertilizer rates) and cropping seasons were simulated for a total of 18 Units of Simulation (USMs).

The model was run using the JavaSTICS software which offers a userfriendly interface to manage the different inputs and outputs, forcing certain variables, and running simulations ([Brisson et al., 2003](#page-14-0)). The software also offers an optimization tool which is used in this study.

#### *2.6. STICS calibration*

The observed data in sole cropping systems were used for all the plant files calibration (i.e. sole chickpea, intercropped chickpea, sole wheat and intercropped wheat). The calibration was carried out using the data collected during both 2018–2019 and 2019–2020 growing seasons. To better assess the quality of the calibration, model evaluation (and not validation) was performed for all calibrated parameters. The temporal dynamic of all outputs variables in sole cropping system were evaluated among the same dataset used for calibration. However, the evaluation of intercropping was performed by using all data collected from intercropped plots in the first and second growing seasons. This makes the intercropping evaluation independent from the data used for its calibration.

# *2.6.1. Chickpea crop*

To our knowledge this work is the first calibration ever conducted for chickpea using the STICS crop model. This calibration procedure was performed using three steps, partially inspired from similar approach adopted on faba bean by [Falconnier et al. (2019).](#page-14-0) The first step consisted of a literature review for the identification of key parameters required by the model. The model was already calibrated for the pea crop. Due to the physiology similarities between the two species we conserved the same formalisms and initial values as the pea plant file ([Corre-Hellou et al.,](#page-14-0)  [2009)](#page-14-0), except for the LAI calculation option, for which we chose to simulate the LAI as the net balance between growth and senescence as fully described by [Brisson et al. (2008)](#page-14-0). We only set the *kmax* parameter from the literature ([Table 3](#page-5-0)) that corresponds to the maximum crop coefficient for water requirements. For chickpea species *kmax* corresponds to 1 according to the [Garofalo et al. (2009).](#page-14-0) In the second step, we set values of parameters directly from the experimental dataset. In total, 4 parameters were determined this way, including minimum and maximum number of fruits per surface area (i.e. *nbgrmin* and *nbgrmax*), maximum grain weight (*pgrainmaxi*) and maximum height of the crop (*hautmax*). The last step consisted of parameter optimization. This step followed the methodology presented by [Guillaume et al. (2011).](#page-14-0) These authors divided the optimization work into different stages, where each stage representing a key process simulated by the model. While the optimized values of the previous stage being used as the default values of the next stage.

We optimized several parameters, which were linked to the different processes taken into account by the model: phenology, roots growth, leaf expansion, shoot growth, partitioning of biomass in organs, N uptake, N fixation and yield formation ([Table 2](#page-4-0)). The optimized parameters in this present study are the most sensitive parameters of the model according to a sensitivity analysis conducted by [Ruget et al. (2002)](#page-15-0). Most calibration works using STICS use this sensitivity analysis as a basis ([Fal](#page-14-0)[connier el al, 2019)](#page-14-0) as well as the methodology we followed in this study and indicated by [Guillaume et al. (2011)](#page-14-0), this explain why we had not to carry out a sensitivity analysis before starting this work. Emergence parameters were not optimized as we forced the model to simulate plant emergence at each observed date. Furthermore, we did not optimize the parameters controlling the frost stress because no frost occurred during the field experiment. Parameter optimization was performed by using the following variables: (i) dates of observed development stages (end of juvenile phase, flowering and harvest stage), (ii) temporal dynamic of plant and soil variables (LAI, root depth, soil water and N contents (0–80 cm depth), above-ground dry biomass, and plant N content) and (iii) yield variables (number of grains per m2 , and grain yield). The optimization was carried out using the Javastics optimization tool, which uses the simplex algorithm ([Nelder-Mead, 1965](#page-15-0)), based on least squares criterion. It is a local and frequentist optimization method that minimizes a criterion in order to adequate the model with the observed values. The Nelder-Mead simplex is a non-linear algorithm used to optimize an objective function in a multidimensional space. The parameter values vary from a starting point and change during the multiple iterations until the values allow having a local optimum for the objective function. This method only needs the criterion value to ensure the optimization and is adapted to non-differentiable function, which is the case of STICS. As a heuristic method, the Nelder-Mead simplex permits to perform a quick optimization. However, the proposed solution may not be optimal as there is a risk to be trapped by local minima. To avoid this risk we used at least three starting points for each of the optimization stages. This method is commonly used for STICS

# *2.6.2. Durum wheat crop*

Durum Wheat is one of the most common calibrated crops by STICS model. It has been calibrated for a wide range of cultivars by several studies ([Brisson et al., 1998b](#page-14-0) and 2002). However, this research is considered as the first study in which STICS calibration was performed on *Vitron* cultivar, which is commonly cultivated in Algeria under Mediterranean conditions. We used the initial values of the *Acalou*  (available in STICS 9.2 version) cultivar as it is the closest calibrated cultivar to *Vitron*. To do this, we followed the same method that was adopted for Chickpea calibration (all formalisms remained the same as in the initial file, and no parameter was determined using literature). The simulated radiation interception was done by using Beer's law for wheat sole crop as well as for the chickpea while the crop coefficient approach was used as presented in [Brisson et al. (2008)](#page-14-0) to tand for crop water requirement. The potential evapotranspiration was calculated using Penman's formula ([Penman, 1948)](#page-15-0).

# *2.6.3. Durum wheat-chickpea Intercropping*

The intercropping calibration was carried out using the sole crops datasets of each species, while the associated crops dataset was only used for model evaluation. The initial values of the plant files correspond to the final values obtained during sole crops calibration. The STICS intercrop model assumes that a simple geometric shape (i.e. rectangle or triangle) can represent a canopy ([Brisson et al., 2008)](#page-14-0). We tested both shapes for the two crops, and the triangle shape gave the best results for both wheat and chickpea canopies. The optimization and simulation procedures of intercropping system were performed by using the radiative transfer formalism. We developed one supplementary stage in optimization procedures for intercrops by using LAI observed values. The optimized parameters that were generated from these procedures were: *ktrou*, *rapforme*, *adfol*, *dfolbas* and *dfolhaut* (see Table 2). The *rsmin*  parameter was also optimized since it is a part of the resistive approach formalism that was activated to simulate crop water requirements in intercropping. The potential evapotranspiration was calculated using the Shuttleworth-Wallace equation.

# *2.7. Statistical analysis and model evaluation*

We evaluated the model both graphically and quantitatively using several statistical indicators: the Root Mean Square Error (RMSE), the Normalized Root Mean Square Error Model (NRMSE), the model Efficiency (EF), and the Pearson correlation coefficient (R2 ). RMSE (Eq. (1)) allows determining the model prediction error, we try to minimize this value as much as possible to maximize the accuracy of the model.

$$\text{RMSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}} \sum \text{ni} = 1 \text{ (Oi} - \text{Si)}^2 \tag{1}$$

NRMSE (Eq. (2)) is the normalized RMSE, which facilitates the comparison when considering different scales.

$$\text{NRMSE} = \frac{\text{RMSE}}{\overline{\text{O}}} \ast 100\tag{2}$$

EF (Eq. (3)) is a valuable indicator to compare model simulations with different parameters set

$$EF = 1 - \frac{\sum n\text{ }i = 1(Oi-\text{Si})^2}{\sum ni = 1(Oi-\text{Si})^2} \tag{3}$$

R2 (Eq. (4)) is used to evaluate the accuracy of the linear regression between observed and simulated values.

$$\mathcal{R}^2 = \quad \left[\frac{\sum \text{n i} = 1 \quad (\text{Si} \text{i} - \text{ }\overline{\text{Si}})(\text{Oi} - \text{ }\overline{\text{Oi}})}{\sigma \text{S} \sigma \text{O}}\right]^2 \tag{4}$$

With Oi and Si being respectively the observed and simulated values for the ith measurements. O and S being the mean of the observed and simulated values, and n the total number of observations. The <span id="page-5-0"></span>combination of these four indicators permitted a detailed evaluation of the model. We used the STICS *eval R* library to compute all plots and statistical criteria presented in this study.
